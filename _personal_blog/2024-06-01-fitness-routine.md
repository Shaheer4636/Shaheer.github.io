There's a version of me from a few years ago who would have laughed at the idea of a consistent workout schedule. Between university deadlines, research projects, and now a full-time engineering role, "consistent" felt like a word that belonged to people with simpler lives.

But somewhere along the way, fitness stopped feeling like something I had to make time for and started feeling like something that makes everything else work better.

---

## The Split: 4 Days Lifting, 3 Days Cardio

My weekly structure looks like this:

**Lifting (4 days):**
- **Day 1** — Push: Chest, shoulders, triceps
- **Day 2** — Pull: Back, biceps, rear delts
- **Day 3** — Legs: Quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves
- **Day 4** — Upper body: A mix of compound and accessory work

**Cardio (3 days):**
- Usually on off days or after a session
- 30–45 minutes — a mix of steady-state (incline walking, cycling) and occasional HIIT when I'm feeling ambitious
- Nothing too extreme — the goal is cardiovascular health and staying lean, not punishment

![In the gym](/images/personal/Fitness2-2024.jpeg)

## Why This Structure Works for Me

**It's not about being optimal. It's about being consistent.**

Seven sessions a week sounds like a lot written down. In practice, it means I'm doing *something* on most days, and that rhythm is what keeps the habit alive. On weeks when work or travel disrupts things, I don't spiral — I just pick back up.

A few principles I've landed on:

- **Morning sessions when possible.** Before the day eats my energy, before I've had a chance to talk myself out of it. Even a 45-minute session at 6 AM before work beats the perfect 90-minute session that never happens.

- **The cardio days are non-negotiable.** I feel the difference in focus and stress levels when I skip them. Running or cycling has become as much a mental reset as a physical one.

- **Progressive overload, not ego lifting.** I track my lifts. Slow, steady progress beats injury every single time.

- **Nutrition follows the training.** I'm not obsessive about macros, but I'm deliberate — enough protein, whole foods most of the time, and I don't eat my feelings after a stressful sprint at work.

---

## The Real Challenge: Getting There on Hard Days

The gym is easy when you're motivated. The discipline is showing up when you're tired after a long day of debugging infrastructure issues or writing research papers.

What's helped me: I stopped making the decision at the moment. The schedule is the decision. At a certain time, I go. I don't negotiate with myself about it anymore — that negotiation is where consistency goes to die.

Some days the session is great. Some days I'm just going through the motions. Both count.

---

## What Fitness Has Done Beyond the Physical

I didn't expect this, but a consistent training routine has genuinely made me better at work. The focus is sharper. The stress tolerance is higher. There's something about the discipline of showing up for your body that carries over into how you approach problems.

If you're in a demanding field — engineering, research, anything that takes a lot of cognitive load — I'd argue that fitness isn't a luxury you fit in around your work. It's part of what lets you do the work at the level you want to.

Start simple. Stay consistent. The rest follows.
