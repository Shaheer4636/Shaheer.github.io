I remember walking into the venue and immediately noticing the energy. It wasn't the usual stiff conference atmosphere — it was genuinely excited. People were there because they wanted to be, because something was happening in the technology world that felt urgent and worth paying attention to.

This was Google I/O Extended, Islamabad, 2023. And it turned out to be one of the more memorable afternoons I've had as someone building a career at the intersection of cloud infrastructure and AI.

---

## What Google I/O Extended Is

For those unfamiliar: Google I/O Extended is a community-run series of events that brings Google I/O content — the annual developer conference — to cities around the world that can't all fly to Mountain View. Local Google Developer Groups (GDGs) organize watch parties, technical sessions, and discussions built around the themes from the main event.

Islamabad's edition in 2023 arrived at a particularly charged moment. The AI conversation had already shifted from academic circles to everywhere, and Google's announcements that year — around Bard, Duet AI, and the deeper integration of generative models into Workspace and Cloud — gave attendees a lot to process and discuss.

---

## The Emerging AI Sessions

The sessions on emerging AI were what drew me in most. The discussions covered:

- **Large language models in production** — not just what they could do, but the real considerations around latency, cost, and reliability when integrating them into actual systems
- **Google's approach to responsible AI** — how guardrails, model cards, and evaluation frameworks were being built into the tooling
- **Multimodal AI** — the move beyond text-only models toward systems that could reason across text, images, and code

What struck me was how quickly the conversation had moved from *"is this real?"* to *"how do we actually use this?"* Engineers and developers in the room were already thinking in implementation terms. The era of AI being a research curiosity felt definitively over.

---

## The Google Cloud Track

The Cloud track was equally interesting — and more directly relevant to the work I was doing at the time with Kubernetes infrastructure at NCP.

Highlights included:
- **GKE (Google Kubernetes Engine) updates** — improvements in autopilot mode and workload isolation
- **Vertex AI** — Google's unified ML platform, and how it was being positioned as the bridge between model development and production deployment
- **Cloud security** — zero-trust approaches, IAM improvements, and how multi-tenant architectures could be hardened in practice

Hearing these discussed in a room of local engineers — people building real systems under real constraints — gave it a grounding that watching a keynote stream never quite does.

---

## The Part That Stayed With Me

The technical content was good. But what I actually took home was something more ambient: the sense of being in a community of people who are paying close attention to a rapidly shifting landscape and are genuinely trying to figure out their place in it.

Pakistan's tech scene is growing fast. The developers in that room weren't passively watching what was being built elsewhere — they were thinking about how to build it here, how to contribute, how to leverage these tools in the specific context of problems worth solving locally and globally.

That kind of room is energizing. It reminds you that what you're working on — however deep in the infrastructure stack you happen to be — is connected to something larger.

---

I left with a few pages of notes, a handful of new connections, and the distinct feeling that the next few years were going to be interesting.

They have been.
